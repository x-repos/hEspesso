\section{Destabilisation of \ch{MgH2} by substitutional Ni and Co dopants:
         a first-principles study}
\label{sec:nicodop}

Pure \ch{MgH2} stores
\SI{7.6}{\percent} hydrogen by mass, but its formation enthalpy
($\Delta H_\mathrm{f} \approx \SI{-75}{\kilo\joule\per\mol}~\ch{H2}$,
Bogdanovi\'c et al.~\cite{bogdanovic1999}) is so negative -- the
hydride so stable -- that release requires temperatures around
\SI{300}{\celsius}. A useful
catalyst for this system is one that destabilises the hydride --
makes $\Delta H$ less negative -- so that release happens at lower
temperature. Nickel in particular is known to improve the hydrogen
sorption properties of \ch{MgH2}~\cite{peng2022}. To provide
a first-principles rationale for the Ni- and Co-doped magnesium
hydrides investigated experimentally in this work, we carried out
density-functional theory (DFT)
calculations of the hydride formation enthalpy. Within a single
internally consistent setup we quantify whether substitutional Ni and
Co at the Mg site destabilise the hydride at a fixed dopant
concentration.

\subsection{Methods}
\label{sec:nicodop:methods}

We compare three hydrogenation pathways on a common per-\ch{H2} basis:
\begin{align}
\ch{Mg}          &+ \ch{H2}      \longrightarrow \ch{MgH2}                \tag{pure}\\
\ch{Mg_{14}Ni_2} &+ 16\,\ch{H2}  \longrightarrow \ch{Mg_{14}Ni_2H_{32}}  \tag{Ni dopant, 12.5\%}\\
\ch{Mg_{14}Co_2} &+ 16\,\ch{H2}  \longrightarrow \ch{Mg_{14}Co_2H_{32}}  \tag{Co dopant, 12.5\%}
\end{align}
The two doped pathways use the same supercell topology -- a
$2\!\times\!2\!\times\!2$ replication of the rutile \ch{MgH2} cell for
the products and the matching $2\!\times\!2\!\times\!2$ hexagonal
close-packed Mg cell for the reactants, with two of the sixteen metal
sites occupied by the dopant \ch{X = Ni, Co}, i.e.\ 12.5 atomic percent
(at.\%) of the metal atoms. The two doped pathways differ only in the
chemical identity of the dopant, and each is referenced to the same
pristine pathway, so the destabilisation $\Delta\Delta H$ relative to
pure \ch{MgH2} benefits from cancellation of supercell-size,
$k$-point-sampling, and symmetry errors.
Figure~\ref{fig:hydrides} shows the three relaxed product structures.

\caption{Relaxed product structures: pristine rutile \ch{MgH2} (left),

atoms occupy diagonally opposite Mg sites in the

All calculations are performed with Quantum ESPRESSO~\cite{giannozzi2017}.
The exchange-correlation functional is the
Perdew--Burke--Ernzerhof (PBE) generalised-gradient approximation,
augmented with Grimme-D3 dispersion~\cite{grimme2010}.
Plane-wave cutoffs are \SI{60}{\rydberg} (wavefunctions) and
\SI{480}{\rydberg} (charge density). Pseudopotentials are of the
projector augmented-wave (PAW) type for Mg and H and Vanderbilt
ultrasoft pseudopotentials (USPP) for Ni and Co. Brillouin-zone sampling uses
$\Gamma$-centred Monkhorst--Pack grids of $12\!\times\!12\!\times\!8$
for the Mg primitive, $8\!\times\!8\!\times\!12$ for rutile \ch{MgH2},
$6\!\times\!6\!\times\!4$ for the Mg supercells, $4\!\times\!4\!\times\!6$
for the hydride supercells, and the $\Gamma$ point for the isolated
\ch{H2} molecule. Metallic phases are described with cold smearing of
width \SI{0.01}{\rydberg}, while the isolated \ch{H2} molecule uses
fixed occupations. The self-consistent field (SCF) cycle is converged
to $\SI{1e-9}{\rydberg}$ using Anderson charge-density mixing. Cell
parameters and atomic positions are relaxed simultaneously with a
quasi-Newton scheme until the residual forces fall below
\SI{1e-4}{\rydberg\per\bohr}. For the doped supercells no crystal
symmetry is imposed during relaxation, so that the small unit-cell
drift accumulated over the optimisation does not interfere with the
symmetry analysis.

For each pathway the per-\ch{H2} formation enthalpy is
\begin{equation}
\Delta H = \frac{1}{n_{\ch{H2}}}\,
   \bigl[\,E(\text{hydride cell}) \;-\; E(\text{metal cell})
                        \;-\; n_{\ch{H2}}\,E(\ch{H2})\bigr]
   \times \SI{1312.75}{\kilo\joule\per\mol\per\rydberg}.
\end{equation}

We use a non-spin-polarised treatment throughout. A spin-polarised
check on \ch{Mg14Ni2} gave \SI{0}{\bohrmagneton} per Ni, so spin
polarisation has no effect on the Ni pathway, and in test runs its
influence on $\Delta H$ was below \SI{1}{\kilo\joule\per\mol}~\ch{H2}.
Cobalt carries a moment of $\sim\!\SI{1.3}{\bohrmagneton}$ per atom in
the metallic reactant that is largely quenched by H bonding in the
hydride; treating reactant and product on the same non-magnetic
footing is expected to cancel most of the resulting bias in $\Delta H$,
and it also removes a spin-related convergence instability that
otherwise prevented the SCF cycle from converging for \ch{Mg14X2H32}.
The residual spin correction for the Co pathway has not been quantified
and is the main uncertainty specific to that pathway, although given
its magnitude relative to $\Delta\Delta H$ it is not expected to alter
the qualitative ordering.

\subsection{DFT results}
\label{sec:nicodop:results}

All seven calculations completed successfully, with the SCF cycle
converged at every step of the relaxation. The geometry optimisation
reached $|\boldsymbol F| < \SI{5e-4}{\rydberg\per\bohr}$ on six
structures; for \ch{Mg14Ni2H32} the relaxation stalled at
$|\boldsymbol F| = \SI{5e-3}{\rydberg\per\bohr}$, but its total energy
was already converged to within one part in $10^{6}$, so the residual
numerical uncertainty is
$\lesssim\!\SI{1}{\kilo\joule\per\mol}~\ch{H2}$. The resulting
formation enthalpies are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:nicodop:dH}.

The pure-Mg baseline ($\Delta H =
\SI{-63.75}{\kilo\joule\per\mol}~\ch{H2}$) sits about
\SI{11}{\kilo\joule\per\mol} above experiment
($\SI{-75}{\kilo\joule\per\mol}~\ch{H2}$~\cite{bogdanovic1999}),
reflecting the known PBE under-binding of hydrides~\cite{pozzo2008}. The
inter-pathway differences $\Delta\Delta H$ are differential
quantities and benefit from cancellation of this systematic
under-binding error, so they are meaningful even at the absolute level
of accuracy delivered here.

Table~\ref{tab:nicodop:dH} shows that both Ni and Co dopants at
12.5\,at.\% destabilise \ch{MgH2} relative to the pristine pathway:
both $\Delta\Delta H$ values are positive and exceed by an order of
magnitude the residual numerical uncertainty of the calculation. Each
dopant therefore makes the hydride easier to dehydrogenate than pure
\ch{MgH2}. This is consistent with the long-standing experimental
finding that Ni-containing magnesium hydrides are less stable than pure
\ch{MgH2}: the dedicated ternary phase \ch{Mg2NiH4}
desorbs with $\Delta H \approx \SI{-64.4}{\kilo\joule\per\mol}~\ch{H2}$~\cite{reilly1968},
some \SI{10}{\kilo\joule\per\mol} less stable than \ch{MgH2}. We
emphasise that this is a comparison of trend only: our \ch{Mg14Ni2H32}
models dilute (12.5\,at.\%) substitution within the rutile \ch{MgH2}
lattice, which is a distinct system from the ordered, Ni-rich
\ch{Mg2NiH4} phase, so the absolute enthalpies are not expected to
coincide.

enough to lower the release temperature without sacrificing storage
capacity, and substitutional Ni or Co does exactly that. These
calculations thus provide a thermodynamic rationale for the
